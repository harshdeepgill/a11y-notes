UI/UX Design Considerations

A designer's-perspective checklist for the UI/UX of websites and components. The overarching rule: never rely on a single sense or cue — pair color with text, text with icons, motion with static fallbacks.

01 Color & Contrast

  • Color must never be the only visual cue. Anything shown by color (errors, required fields, selected states, links, chart segments) needs a second indicator — text, icon, underline, pattern, or shape. SC 1.4.1
  • Text contrast must be at least 4.5:1 against its background (3:1 for large text). SC 1.4.3
  • Non-text contrast — buttons, form borders, icons, UI boundaries — must be at least 3:1. SC 1.4.11
  • Design for Forced Colors / High Contrast Mode: use borders (not just background fills) for boundaries, since backgrounds and background-image icons can disappear.

02 Focus & Keyboard Experience

  • Every interactive element needs a clearly visible focus indicator — it's the keyboard user's cursor. Design it intentionally; don't remove it.
  • Focus indicators should enclose the element, be thick enough to notice, and have 3:1 contrast against both the element and its surroundings. A "halo" (light gap + colored ring) works on any background. SC 2.4.7, 2.4.11
  • Design with a logical reading / tab order in mind — visual layout should match the order people move through content.

03 Typography & Text

  • Use real text, not images of text. Images of text don't scale, recolor, or translate. SC 1.4.5
  • Layouts must survive 200% zoom / text resize without losing content or breaking. SC 1.4.4
  • Choose highly legible fonts (e.g. Atkinson Hyperlegible, built for character recognition).

04 Headings & Structure

  • If it looks like a heading, it must be a heading — and visual size should reflect true hierarchy.
  • Don't skip heading levels for styling reasons; visual and semantic hierarchy must align.
  • Design clear landmark regions (header, nav, main, footer, sidebar) so users can orient and navigate. Keep them few and purposeful.

05 Forms

  • Labels must be visible and persistent — never use placeholder text as the label (it disappears, has low contrast, and confuses users). SC 3.3.2
  • Indicate required fields with more than an asterisk or color — pair it with text like "(required)", or, if you do use an asterisk, state up front (e.g. at the top of the form) that "Fields marked with * are required."
  • Group related fields visually and semantically (address blocks, radio sets) with a group heading / legend.
  • Error messages must be specific and suggest a fix — "Enter a date as MM/DD/YYYY," not just "Invalid." SC 3.3.1, 3.3.3
  • Collect all errors in one summary at the top. When a submitted form has errors, show a single container that lists every error — each item linking to the field it refers to — and move focus to it, instead of making people hunt for scattered inline messages. SC 3.3.1
  • Don't validate too aggressively (avoid erroring the moment someone leaves a field); give people a chance to finish.
  • For important / legal / financial actions, allow review, confirm, or undo. SC 3.3.4

06 Labels, Naming & Repeated Controls

  • The visible label must match the accessible name — voice-control users speak what they see. Avoid a button that reads "Search" but is named "Submit query." SC 2.5.3
  • Make repeated controls distinguishable. A page of identical "Read more" / "Add to cart" buttons is confusing — add context ("Read more about [Article Title]").
  • Icon-only buttons need a text label (visible or accessible) — an icon alone isn't enough.

07 Motion, Cognition & Inclusive Patterns

  • Design safer animation for motion-sensitive users; respect reduced-motion preferences and avoid excessive auto-movement.
  • Reduce cognitive load with progressive disclosure (accordions, "show more") — but note that exclusive accordions (only one open) prevent comparing sections and can frustrate users.
  • Offer choices where possible — e.g. letting people pick how they're contacted (text, voice, custom). Going beyond minimum compliance creates genuinely inclusive experiences.

08 Feedback & Notifications

  • Transient messages must reach everyone. A toast, snackbar, or "Added to cart" confirmation that only flashes visually is invisible to screen-reader users — design these so the update is announced, not just shown. SC 4.1.3
  • Match urgency to interruption. Reserve interrupting ("assertive") announcements for critical, time-sensitive messages (session timeout, payment failure); routine success / status updates should be polite and non-interrupting. Don't over-notify.

09 Navigation & Orientation

  • Provide a way to skip repeated blocks (skip-to-content) so people don't tab through the same nav on every page. SC 2.4.1
  • Give each page a unique, descriptive title that matches its main heading — it's the first orientation cue. SC 2.4.2
  • Convey current state clearly (current page in nav, selected tab, pressed toggle) — visually and not by color alone.

10 Touch & Mobile

  • Ensure touch targets are large enough and that controls sit where they visually appear — mobile screen-reader users explore by dragging a finger to where things look like they are.

★ The Overarching Mindset

  • WCAG is the floor, not the ceiling. Meeting criteria doesn't guarantee a usable experience — design for real people and, ideally, test with disabled users.
  • Don't rely on a single sense or cue. Pair color with text, text with icons, motion with static fallbacks — redundancy is inclusive design.
